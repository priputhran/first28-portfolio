01 — Research foundation

Grounded in clinical research, expanded with current evidence

This project began with a 2018 UC Davis paper proposing a mobile app for neonatal health risk assessment. Rather than treat it as a brief, I treated it as a baseline — then went looking for what the evidence said in 2024.

The synthesis across five research areas reshaped key product decisions: day-specific risk thresholds, EPDS integration as a first-class feature, offline-first as a health equity requirement, and a clear non-provider boundary enforced in architecture.

02 — Product strategy

Time-boxing is the killer feature

Most parenting apps are unbounded — usable for years, generic by necessity. First 28's hard 28-day constraint creates urgency, clinical focus, and a clear definition of success. The app has a job to do, does it, and graduates the user.

Three strategic bets drove prioritisation: the safety loop (triage + provider alert) for clinical credibility, breastfeeding coaching for daily active use, and the provider dashboard for B2B distribution.

Must have

Symptom checker + triage

15-rule decision engine with GREEN / AMBER / RED output. Offline-capable, FHIR-backed.

Must have

Growth + vitals dashboard

Weight, feeds, wet diapers, temperature. Visual trend chart. Offline entry with sync.

Should have

Provider alert dashboard

Nurse-facing risk flags, image submissions, missed check-ins. Bi-directional messaging.

Should have

EPDS mental health check-in

Daily micro-survey. Q10 bypasses LLM with hardcoded safety resource. Provider notified.

03 — AI triage architecture

The AI is a narrator, not a clinician

The most important architectural decision: the LLM's role is narrowly scoped to translating structured signal scores into plain-language sentences. It never sees raw user input, never assigns a tier, and never produces diagnostic language.

Five layers enforce the non-provider boundary: a clinician-reviewed system prompt, structured context injection (no free text), a JSON output schema, a regex-based output validator, and hardcoded fallback responses for every tier.

"A single RED rule always wins — there is no averaging. One red signal beats many amber signals."

04 — UX principles

Designed for the 2am brain

The primary user is sleep-deprived, emotionally heightened, and one-handed. Every design decision flows from a single constraint: this app must be completable in under 60 seconds, in the dark, holding a baby.

P1

One action per screen

Every screen has exactly one primary CTA. Secondary actions are de-emphasised to near-invisibility. No decision paralysis at 3am.

P2

Structured input, conversational feel

The backend needs typed fields. The mom sees one question at a time, with tap options. No free text, no forms — it feels like a concerned friend asking.

P3

Amber, not red, for "contact care team"

Standard medical red triggers panic. Amber says "action needed today." Red is reserved for the single emergency screen where the only action is calling 911.

P4

Escalation is frictionless; de-escalation is not

Moving from green → red requires zero friction. Clearing a red tier requires provider confirmation. Easy to take action seriously; intentionally harder to dismiss concern.

P5

Progress without punishment

Streak mechanics celebrate logging without punishing missed days. No red X marks. No "you haven't checked in for 18 hours!" guilt notifications.

P6

Offline-first is a health equity feature

In low-resource settings, offline isn't an edge case — it's the default. No "connect to internet" dead-ends on any core flow. Data syncs silently when available.

The anti-metric insight

Don't optimise for daily opens

A mom opening the app 8 times a day on day 14 is not a retention success — it's anxiety spiralling. Monitor the ratio of check-ins to opens. It should be close to 1:1. If DAU spikes without a corresponding triage event, the app is feeding worry rather than resolving it.

Prompts are code, not config

The system prompt lives in version control, has its own PR review process, and requires dual sign-off from engineering and a clinical reviewer before merge. Hotfixes to production prompts are not permitted.

The red-team suite is append-only

Every new failure mode found — in testing, in production, or in clinical review — becomes a permanent test case. The suite is institutional memory. It only grows, never shrinks.

06 — Phased roadmap

Three bets, eighteen months

The roadmap is structured around three distribution milestones: individual mom validation, hospital system partnership, and clinical outcomes publication — each unlocking the next.

Phase 1 · Months 0–4

Foundation — validated MVP

  • Triage engine with 15 rules (offline)
  • Breastfeeding + diaper + weight logger
  • FHIR R4 backend
  • Formative usability testing, 20 new moms
Phase 2 · Months 5–9

Intelligence layer

  • Computer vision for jaundice / skin assessment
  • Provider alert dashboard
  • EPDS daily mental health check-in
  • Pragmatic trial launch with partner hospital
Phase 3 · Months 10–18

Scale + ecosystem

  • EHR integration (Epic FHIR API)
  • Multi-language expansion (4+ languages)
  • B2B licensing to hospital systems and NGOs
  • Outcomes study publication
