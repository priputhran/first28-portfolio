01 - Research foundation

Started in 2018. Finished with better tools.

I was part of the team that wrote the original paper at UC Davis. We identified the gap, designed the decision tree by hand, and published the research. What we couldn't do then was to build the intelligent layer the product really needed - the AI capabilities simply weren't there yet.

Coming back to it in 2026 meant asking a harder question: not just what did we propose, but what does the evidence actually say now? That updated synthesis - across neonatal mortality timing, mHealth efficacy, maternal mental health, and breastfeeding support - reshaped key product decisions: day-specific risk thresholds, EPDS as a first-class feature, offline-first as a health equity requirement, and a hard non-provider boundary enforced in the architecture itself.

02 - Product strategy

From decision tree to intelligent triage

The original 2018 prototype used a decision table we built by hand - mapping symptom combinations to risk levels based on WHO and AAP guidelines. It worked, but it was brittle: rigid logic, no natural language, no way to explain its reasoning to a worried mother at 2am.

The 2026 version keeps that deterministic core - every triage decision is still made by an explicit, auditable rule - but adds an LLM layer whose sole job is to translate the rule output into warm, plain-language guidance. The logic is transparent. The language is human. And the non-provider boundary is enforced at the architecture level, not just in the prompt.

Must have

Symptom checker + triage

15-rule decision engine with GREEN / AMBER / RED output. Offline-capable, FHIR-backed.

Must have

Growth + vitals dashboard

Weight, feeds, wet diapers, temperature. Visual trend chart. Offline entry with sync.

Should have

Provider alert dashboard

Nurse-facing risk flags, image submissions, missed check-ins. Bi-directional messaging.

Should have

EPDS mental health check-in

Daily micro-survey. Q10 bypasses LLM with hardcoded safety resource. Provider notified.

03 - AI triage architecture

The AI is a narrator, not a decision-maker

The most important architectural decision: the LLM's role is narrowly scoped to translating structured signal scores into plain-language sentences. It never sees raw user input, never assigns a tier, and never produces diagnostic language.

The deterministic triage engine is a direct evolution of the decision table from the original 2018 paper - now 15 explicit, human-readable rules, each with a source citation, each auditable without opening a model. The LLM only gets involved after the tier is already decided. Its job is translation, not reasoning.

"The original decision tree was the right instinct. The 2026 version keeps that auditable logic at its core - the LLM only narrates what the rules have already decided."

On the design of the triage engine
# System prompt - clinician sign-off required to change YOU ARE NOT: - A doctor, nurse, midwife, or licensed provider - Able to diagnose any condition or disease - Able to recommend any medication or dosage - Able to give an "all-clear" YOUR ONLY JOB: observed_summary: 2 sentences, plain language, no medical terms watch_list: 2–4 specific observable things to monitor

The 15 triage rules - what they watch for
Feeding (5 rules)
Fewer than 6 feeds in 24h - feed gap over 5h - fewer than 3 wet diapers - weight loss after day 5 - projectile vomiting
Breathing (3 rules)
Grunting, flaring, or retractions - breathing rate over 60 per min at rest - blue or grey tinge around lips
Skin (3 rules)
Yellow tint on face - yellow spreading to chest or soles - photo CV model confidence over 60%
Temp + wellbeing (4 rules)
Temp below 36.5C - fever above 38C (always red) - EPDS score 13 or above - EPDS Q10 positive (bypasses AI entirely)
One RED rule always wins - there is no averaging. Rules are based on WHO, AAP, and NICE neonatal guidelines and are written to be human-readable in the source code.
04 - UX principles

Designed for the 2am brain

The primary user is sleep-deprived, emotionally heightened, and one-handed. Every design decision flows from a single constraint: this app must be completable in under 60 seconds, in the dark, holding a baby.

P1

One action per screen

Every screen has exactly one primary CTA. Secondary actions are de-emphasised to near-invisibility. No decision paralysis at 3am.

P2

Structured input, conversational feel

The backend needs typed fields. The mom sees one question at a time, with tap options. No free text, no forms - it feels like a concerned friend asking.

P3

Amber, not red, for "contact care team"

Standard medical red triggers panic. Amber says "action needed today." Red is reserved for the single emergency screen where the only action is calling 911.

P4

Escalation is frictionless; de-escalation is not

Moving from green → red requires zero friction. Clearing a red tier requires provider confirmation. Easy to take action seriously; intentionally harder to dismiss concern.

P5

Progress without punishment

Streak mechanics celebrate logging without punishing missed days. No red X marks. No "you haven't checked in for 18 hours!" guilt notifications.

P6

Offline-first is a health equity feature

In low-resource settings, offline isn't an edge case - it's the default. No "connect to internet" dead-ends on any core flow. Data syncs silently when available.

05 - What was built

From decision tree to deployable code

The hand-crafted decision table from 2018 is now a 15-rule Python engine - explicit, auditable, version-controlled. Wrapped around it: a FastAPI backend, an LLM narration layer with a hard non-provider boundary, and a React Native mobile frontend. The codebase includes a CI pipeline and 18 adversarial safety tests designed to run on every commit.

The anti-metric insight

Don't optimise for daily opens

A mom opening the app 8 times a day on day 14 is not a retention success - it's anxiety spiralling. Monitor the ratio of check-ins to opens. It should be close to 1:1. If DAU spikes without a corresponding triage event, the app is feeding worry rather than resolving it.

Prompts are code, not config

The system prompt lives in version control, has its own PR review process, and requires dual sign-off from engineering and a clinical reviewer before merge. Hotfixes to production prompts are not permitted.

The red-team suite is append-only

Every new failure mode found - in testing, in production, or in clinical review - becomes a permanent test case. The suite is institutional memory. It only grows, never shrinks.

06 - Phased roadmap

Three bets, eighteen months

The roadmap is structured around three distribution milestones: individual mom validation, hospital system partnership, and clinical outcomes publication - each unlocking the next.

Phase 1 · Months 0–4

Foundation - validated MVP

  • Triage engine with 15 rules (offline)
  • Breastfeeding + diaper + weight logger
  • FHIR R4 backend
  • Formative usability testing, 20 new moms
Phase 2 · Months 5–9

Intelligence layer

  • Computer vision for jaundice / skin assessment
  • Provider alert dashboard
  • EPDS daily mental health check-in
  • Pragmatic trial launch with partner hospital
Phase 3 · Months 10–18

Scale + ecosystem

  • EHR integration (Epic FHIR API)
  • Multi-language expansion (4+ languages)
  • B2B licensing to hospital systems and NGOs
  • Outcomes study publication
